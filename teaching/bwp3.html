BWP3 Information und Gesellschaft

Prof. Dinneen • Wintersemester 2020-2021

Description

This course enables students to reflect on the role of technology in today’s information society from various social, societal, and ethical perspectives, and to consider what information professionals and scholars can do to affect, and hopefully improve, such conditions.

Delivery Modes and Languages

BWP3 consists of seminars (HS) on Tuesday afternoons and lectures (VL) on Wednesday afternoons, each starting at 12:30 and ending by 14:00. The course will be conducted via synchronous online delivery, but includes flexibility for asynchronous study as needed. One link to the Zoom session for all VL & HS is available on the course Moodle page.

The VL consist primarily of content delivered by the professor or guest lecturers and integrated class discussion via voice, video, and/or textual chat messages (as the student prefers). Recordings of the lectures will be provided, but students are encouraged to attend whenever possible.

The HS consist of activities to (1) synthesise ideas from the previous week’s lecture via discussion and student presentations, (2) establish context for new course themes, or (3) prepare for module assessment. This scheduling maximises the time students have to consider the lecture content and prepare for next week’s seminar.

Language requirements

The course will be conducted primarily in English, but with flexibility to accommodate students’ varied linguistic backgrounds and skills/challenges, which are also considered in the design and delivery of course materials and in the review of assessments. In general, linguistic performance will not be assessed, and students are therefore encouraged to participate in English as much as possible. For guidance on particular assessments, please see the relevant section below.

VL Participation

Students should attend lectures and participate in discussions whenever possible via voice or text chat (English/German). If necessary, students may instead watch the lecture recordings and post thoughtful text comments (English/German) on the lecture recordings to extend the class discussion. Such comments must be: on topic, respectful, coherent, no more than 100 words in length, and posted before the following lecture video is uploaded. VL participation is pass/fail, i.e. discussing or posting according to the requirements for all module weeks achieves full LP.

HS Participation

Students should attend seminars and participate in activities / give presentations (English/German) whenever possible. If necessary, students may instead perform the activities and upload presentation videos asynchronously.

  • Presentations should briefly synthesise one narrow topic from the VL of the week prior, and then explain and justify a position / opinion with regards to that topic and with reference to relevant scholarly literature and/or reputable press. Presentations should last 10 minutes (a script may be used), end with a clear takeaway, and then pose 3-5 questions for class discussion. The presenter should then lead/moderate discussion (or briefly respond to comments if posting a video). Video presentations must be uploaded by the start of the relevant HS. The presentation schedule will be decided at the start of the course, but 2 presentations per student is likely (e.g. one before the Winterpause and one after). HS participation is pass/fail, i.e. presenting and participating according to the requirements achieves full LP.
  • Presentations should be accompanied by prepared slides conveying the main points in the language not presented: German slides if you present in English, English slides if you present in German.

Fachartikel

The Fachartikel (English/German) is an essay with original synthesis and argumentation using suitable evidence and reasoning to support a coherent, but not necessarily novel, point / opinion about one course topic (or two if inter-related). After a very brief introduction the author should deeply analyse the chosen topic(s), and then end by discussing what, if anything, the LIS field can do to have an impact on the topic, the public perception of / participation in it, etc. The article should be ca. 6.000 to 10.000 characters (excluding spaces) and therefore ca. 3 to 5 pages. Because clarity is so important in persuasive writing, it is recommended that students write in whichever language they are most comfortable.

Readings and Resources

Preparation for the course requires reading before the lectures and preparing for presentations and participation in the seminars. Readings and resources will be provided on Moodle.
